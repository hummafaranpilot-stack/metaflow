8. Facebook Ads Compliance Guide

Facebook has strict policies for health and supplement advertising. Follow these guidelines to keep your ads approved and your account in good standing. Non-compliance can result in ad rejection, account restrictions, or permanent bans.

What to Avoid (Will Get Your Ad Rejected)

  • Direct health claims or guarantees of results. Never say the product will "cure," "treat," "fix," or "eliminate" any condition. Facebook flags these as medical claims.
    "PowerX Pro cures BPH and eliminates prostate problems.""PowerX Pro supports healthy prostate function with 6 natural ingredients."
  • Before/after implications. Avoid language or imagery that implies a guaranteed transformation. Facebook is especially strict about before-and-after scenarios in health advertising.
  • "You" statements about personal health conditions. Facebook flags ads that call out users' health issues directly, as this violates their personal attributes policy.
    "Are YOU struggling with an enlarged prostate?""Many men over 40 experience changes in prostate health."
  • References to specific diseases or diagnoses. Do not mention cancer, BPH by its clinical name in a treatment context, or any specific medical diagnosis as something the product addresses.
  • Fake urgency or scarcity. Claims like "Only 12 bottles left!" or "Sale ends in 2 hours!" when they are not true can trigger both Facebook policy violations and FTC scrutiny.
  • Misleading imagery. Avoid using images that imply the product is a prescription drug, that depict nudity or sexual situations, or that show unrealistic results.
  • Fake testimonials. All testimonials must be from real users with genuine experiences. The FTC requires that testimonials reflect typical results, or you must clearly disclose that they do not.

Safe Language Patterns

  • "Supports" instead of "fixes" or "cures." Always frame the product as nutritional support, not a medical treatment.
    "Supports healthy prostate function"
    "Helps support normal urinary patterns"
    "Provides nutritional support for prostate health"
  • Third-person framing instead of "you." Instead of calling out the reader directly, speak in general terms about men who experience these issues.
    "Many men over 40 notice changes in urinary patterns."
    "For men experiencing frequent nighttime trips to the bathroom..."
  • Qualify claims with hedge language. Words like "may," "can help," "designed to," and "traditionally used" keep your copy compliant.
    "Saw Palmetto has been traditionally used to support prostate health."
    "These ingredients may help support healthy urinary function."
  • Focus on ingredients, not outcomes. Talk about what is in the formula and the research behind the ingredients rather than promising specific results to the user.
  • Use educational framing. Ads that educate about prostate health generally, and then introduce the product as one option, tend to pass review more easily than direct-sell ads.

Disclaimer Requirements

  • FDA disclaimer on landing pages. Every landing page must include: "These statements have not been evaluated by the Food and Drug Administration. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."
  • "Individual results may vary" should appear near any testimonial or personal experience story, both in the ad copy and on the landing page.
  • Affiliate disclosure. If you are promoting as an affiliate, you must disclose the relationship. FTC requires clear and conspicuous disclosure that you may earn a commission.
  • "Consult your doctor" language should be included when discussing health conditions or when suggesting someone change their health routine.
  • Landing page congruence. Facebook requires that your ad copy, creative, and landing page all tell the same story. Do not use a mild ad that leads to an aggressive landing page, as this is a common reason for account bans.

Pro Tips for Staying Approved

  • Warm up your ad account. Start with small budgets ($5-10/day) and educational content before scaling to direct-response supplement ads. New accounts running aggressive health ads immediately are more likely to get flagged.
  • Use a pre-sell / advertorial page. Instead of sending traffic directly to the sales page, use a pre-sell article that educates about prostate health and naturally introduces PowerX Pro. This improves both compliance and conversion rates.
  • Test your landing page with Facebook's ad review in mind. Run a Page Post Engagement campaign first with a small budget to see if the landing page triggers any issues before committing to a larger Conversion campaign.
  • Avoid "clickbait" formatting in images. Red circles, arrows, shocked faces, and sensationalized thumbnails increase the chance of manual review and rejection.
  • Keep a clean Facebook Page. Your Page should have legitimate posts, a real profile picture, contact information, and a privacy policy link. Pages that only exist to run ads get flagged more often.
  • Appeal rejections with specifics. If your ad is rejected, do not just resubmit it unchanged. Read the specific policy cited, adjust your copy, and submit a detailed appeal explaining how your revised ad complies.
